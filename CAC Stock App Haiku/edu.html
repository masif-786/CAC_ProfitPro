Financial Education Center

Master the fundamentals of finance with our comprehensive glossary

Stock

A share of ownership in a company that represents a claim on part of the corporation's assets and earnings.

Example: Owning one share of Apple (AAPL) stock means you own a tiny piece of the company.

Dividend

A portion of a company's earnings that is paid out to shareholders, usually on a quarterly basis.

Example: If you own 100 shares of a stock paying $2 annual dividend, you'll receive $200 per year.

Compound Interest

Interest earned on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods.

Example: $1,000 at 10% compound interest becomes $1,610.51 after 5 years.

Mutual Fund

A professionally managed investment fund that pools money from many investors to purchase securities.

Example: Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VFIAX) tracks the S&P 500 index.

Diversification

The practice of spreading investments across different assets to reduce risk.

Example: Investing in stocks, bonds, real estate, and commodities instead of just one asset type.

Bear Market

A market condition in which prices of securities fall 20% or more from recent highs.

Example: The stock market entered a bear market in 2022 as the S&P 500 fell over 20%.

Bull Market

A market condition in which prices are rising or expected to rise.

Example: The 2009-2020 bull market saw the S&P 500 rise over 400%.

P/E Ratio

Price-to-Earnings ratio, a metric used to value a company by measuring its current share price relative to earnings per share.

Example: A P/E ratio of 20 means investors are willing to pay $20 for $1 of company earnings.

IRA

Individual Retirement Account - a tax-advantaged investment account for retirement savings.

Example: Contributing $6,000 annually to a Roth IRA from age 25 to 65 could grow to over $1 million.

Asset Allocation

The strategy of dividing investments among different asset categories like stocks, bonds, and cash.

Example: A 60/40 portfolio has 60% in stocks and 40% in bonds.
